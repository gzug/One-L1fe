# Weekly Self-Report Anchors V1

## Verdict

Weekly self-report scores are useful in V1, but only if the scale is anchored.
Without anchors, a `7` this week and a `7` next month may mean different things.

This document defines simple anchor language for the four weekly pillars:
- Exercise
- Sleep
- Nutrition
- Emotional Health

The goal is not clinical precision.
The goal is better consistency over time.

## General Rule

Use the same mental standard each week.
The score should reflect the last 7 days, not one unusually good or bad day.

Recommended anchor points:
- 0 = extremely poor / near-total breakdown
- 3 = clearly bad and limiting
- 5 = mixed / okay but not good
- 7 = solid / generally working
- 9 = very strong / consistently supportive
- 10 = excellent and unusually hard to sustain

## Exercise Anchor

### 0
No meaningful movement, no training, very low activity.

### 3
Some movement happened, but clearly below intended level.
Training was inconsistent or weak.

### 5
Basic activity happened.
Some training or movement, but not enough to feel solid.

### 7
Good week.
Planned movement mostly happened.
Strength, cardio, or general activity supported health.

### 9
Very strong week.
Training was consistent, well-tolerated, and clearly aligned with goals.

### 10
Exceptional week.
High consistency and quality, likely not realistic every week.

## Sleep Anchor

### 0
Sleep was severely disrupted.
Very poor duration, quality, or recovery.

### 3
Sleep was clearly a problem this week.
Too short, poor quality, or badly timed on multiple days.

### 5
Mixed sleep week.
Some nights were okay, but recovery was not consistently strong.

### 7
Good sleep week.
Most nights were supportive, recovery was mostly acceptable.

### 9
Very strong sleep week.
Consistent timing, strong recovery, and good sleep quality.

### 10
Exceptional sleep week.
Very strong across timing, quality, and recovery, hard to sustain continuously.

## Nutrition Anchor

### 0
Nutrition was chaotic or strongly misaligned with goals.

### 3
Food choices were frequently unhelpful.
Structure, quality, or consistency were poor.

### 5
Mixed nutrition week.
Some good meals and some weak ones.
Not clearly bad, not clearly strong.

### 7
Good nutrition week.
Food quality and structure mostly supported goals.

### 9
Very strong nutrition week.
Consistent choices, good structure, and strong alignment with goals.

### 10
Exceptional nutrition week.
Very high consistency and quality, beyond normal sustainable baseline.

## Emotional Health Anchor

### 0
Severe distress or dysfunction.
The week felt emotionally unmanageable.

### 3
Emotional strain was clearly limiting.
Stress, mood, or mental load repeatedly interfered with daily life.

### 5
Mixed emotional week.
Manageable overall, but not stable or clearly good.

### 7
Good emotional week.
Stress was manageable and recovery/support was mostly present.

### 9
Very strong emotional week.
Good stability, resilience, and recovery capacity.

### 10
Exceptional emotional week.
Very high steadiness and support, unlikely to be the normal baseline every week.

## Trend Label Guidance

Trend labels should be simple in V1.

Suggested labels:
- Improving
- Stable
- Declining
- Unknown

### Suggested logic
- Improving: current score is above the recent reference
- Stable: current score is near the recent reference
- Declining: current score is below the recent reference
- Unknown: not enough history

V1 should prefer simple and explainable trend logic over clever trend math.

## Beginner Explanation

Old weekly scoring had a good idea, but the numbers could drift.
A `6` might mean one thing in March and something else in June.

The new version fixes that by giving each number a simple meaning.
That makes weekly trends more believable and easier to compare over time.
